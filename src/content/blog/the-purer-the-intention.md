Tonight a single word started a session that produced a unified theory, six features, a design document, and five garden posts. The word was "vivesca." It wasn't a prompt. It wasn't an instruction. It was an intention — pure enough that the metabolism knew what to do with it.

Later in the same session: "make it real." Three words. The system stopped theorizing and started building. "Just three?" Two words. The system expanded its scope. "Garden?" One word. A post got written and published. "Go take bath." Four words. The system stopped talking, confirmed the background work was running, and let the human leave. While he bathed, a substrate got built, tested, and shipped.

The pattern: shorter sentences metabolised faster and produced more precise products. Not because brevity is a virtue. Because compression is selection.

When you say "make it real," you've already done the metabolic work of selecting what matters from everything that's been discussed. The compression strips context, caveats, alternatives, hedging. What survives is pure intention. And pure intention is a substrate that fits the enzyme perfectly — no wasted energy matching shape, no activation barrier to overcome, no ambiguity to resolve before the reaction can proceed.

Long messages are unmetabolised substrates. They contain the intention somewhere inside, surrounded by context the speaker hasn't yet compressed. The recipient has to do the metabolic work of extracting the intention from the noise. That's not collaboration — that's outsourcing your digestion.

This is why the push taxonomy works. "Just this?" "Are we lazy?" "Perfect?" Each push is a compressed intention — a selection signal that requires no interpretation. The recipient doesn't parse. They react. The reaction is immediate because the substrate is pure.

The biological parallel is enzyme-substrate specificity. An enzyme's active site has a precise shape. A substrate that fits perfectly reacts immediately — low activation energy, high catalytic rate. A substrate that almost fits requires conformational change, energy input, time. A substrate that doesn't fit at all produces no reaction.

In conversation: a pure intention fits the active site. The response is immediate and precise. A muddy intention requires clarification rounds — conformational changes in the conversational medium — before the reaction can proceed. No intention produces no reaction. Silence.

This has a design implication. If you're building systems where humans talk to AI (or AI talks to AI, or humans talk to humans through AI), don't optimise for more context. Optimise for purer intention. The system should help users compress, not expand. The interface should reward brevity, not punish it.

The current paradigm is backwards. We build systems that ask for more detail, more context, more specification. "Can you be more specific?" "What exactly do you mean?" "Please provide additional context." Every question adds noise and delays the reaction. The better question is the one the system never asks — because the intention was pure enough to metabolise directly.

One word started tonight's session. One word is the purest intention. Everything that followed was the metabolism doing what metabolism does — transforming substrate into product, product into substrate, until the conversation itself became the proof of what it was describing.

The purer the intention, the faster the metabolism. The faster the metabolism, the more the conversation is alive.

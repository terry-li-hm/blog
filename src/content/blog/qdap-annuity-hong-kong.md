Hong Kong's Qualifying Deferred Annuity Policy (QDAP) is frequently sold on the strength of its tax benefit: contribute up to HK$60,000 per year and receive a salaries tax deduction worth roughly HK$10,200 annually at the top marginal rate. That is real money, and the pitch is understandable. But the tax saving is only half the story — and the other half changes the conclusion.

## The annuity itself is a poor investment vehicle

Verified across major HK insurers, QDAP products offer guaranteed IRRs of 1% to 3.3% per year. Hang Seng's eIncomePro, one of the market's better offerings, tops out at 3.3%. Most products sit in the 1–2.5% range. After accounting for the insurer's spread — the profit margin embedded in the pricing — policyholders are receiving a return well below what actuarially fair pricing would imply.

But the more important number is the *real* return — what you earn after inflation. Hong Kong inflation runs at roughly 2.5% per year historically. At a 2% QDAP IRR, your money is not growing in real terms. It is shrinking, slowly, year after year. The tax saving flatters the headline; inflation exposes what is underneath.

## The thing most QDAP conversations leave out

Here is the critical detail that most QDAP pitches omit: the HK$60,000 annual tax deduction cap is shared between QDAP premiums and MPF Tax Deductible Voluntary Contributions (TVC). They are not separate allowances — it is one pool.

This means every dollar of tax saving available through QDAP is equally available through an MPF top-up, with one important difference: TVC allows the underlying funds to be invested in equities.

Route HK$60,000 per year into QDAP and the tax saving is HK$10,200. Route the same HK$60,000 into MPF TVC invested in a low-fee global equity index fund, and the tax saving is identical — but the money grows at equity market rates rather than annuity rates.

## The numbers over 23 years

Modelling HK$60K/year from age 37 to 60, assuming 7% gross equity returns:

| Route | Annual fee | Terminal value (per HK$100) |
|---|---|---|
| QDAP | Embedded in IRR | HK$340–410K |
| VWRA only, no tax saving | 0.22% | ~HK$375K |
| MPF TVC, DIS Core Accumulation Fund | 0.75% (capped) | ~HK$403K |

MPF TVC into a low-fee index fund wins — capturing the full tax saving without surrendering equity returns to insurer spreads. There is one important caveat: TVC only beats QDAP if your MPF scheme offers a fund below roughly 0.8% in annual charges. Most schemes offer the DIS Core Accumulation Fund, which is fee-capped by regulation and invests in a globally diversified mix of equities and bonds. If your scheme only has expensive actively managed funds with heavy Hong Kong bias, the fee drag eats the tax benefit.

The picture is about to improve further. Hong Kong's eMPF platform is currently rolling out, consolidating the industry and forcing fee compression across all schemes. The case for TVC strengthens as fees fall.

## Why 100% equities is not as reckless as it sounds

The standard argument for holding fixed income is to provide ballast when equity markets fall. But for a high-income professional with a long career runway, that argument has a flaw: you already hold a massive implicit bond — your future earnings. Discounted over two decades, that stream of income is worth millions in present value terms. The stabilising role that bonds play in a retirement portfolio is already being filled by your paycheck. Your financial portfolio does not need to duplicate it.

This is why aggressive equity allocation makes sense in your 30s — it is the rational response to your full balance sheet, not just the invested portion.

## The right equity instrument — VWRA, not VT

For the equity portion of the portfolio outside of MPF, instrument choice is not a footnote. VT (Vanguard Total World Stock ETF) is the most commonly recommended global equity fund, but it is US-domiciled. Hong Kong residents holding US-domiciled securities face two structural disadvantages: a 30% withholding tax on dividends (no HK-US tax treaty), and US estate tax of up to 40% on assets above approximately USD 60K upon death. On a USD 200K portfolio, that estate tax exposure is USD 56K gone — not a tail risk, a near-certainty if the investor dies before withdrawing.

The Ireland-domiciled equivalent, VWRA, provides near-identical exposure across roughly 3,700 companies in 47 countries at a 15% withholding rate and zero US estate tax exposure. For HK investors, VWRA is strictly the better instrument. This is not a minor optimisation — compounded over decades it represents a material difference in after-tax wealth.

For those willing to go one step further, the academic evidence on factor investing — specifically the small-cap value premium documented by Fama and French — suggests that tilting toward smaller, cheaper companies has historically added 1–2% per year over long horizons. Ireland-domiciled UCITS options like ZPRV (US small-cap value) and ZPRX (European small-cap value) provide this exposure within a tax-efficient wrapper. For a 20+ year horizon, implementing the tilt early compounds meaningfully.

## Planning the transition

The answer is not "go all equity forever." As retirement approaches, the calculation changes. Around age 45–48, it makes sense to begin a gradual de-risking — shifting a portion of the equity portfolio toward bonds or lower-volatility assets, reducing exposure to the sequence-of-returns risk that hits hardest in the final years before retirement. A bad market at 52 is far more damaging than one at 37, because there is less time to recover.

This is also when QDAP becomes worth reconsidering. In the early 50s, the compounding horizon has shortened enough that the guaranteed income floor starts to outweigh the opportunity cost of the low IRR. The longevity hedge — income you cannot outlive regardless of market conditions — becomes genuinely valuable. At 37, you are paying for that guarantee two decades before you need it. At 52, you are buying it at the right time.

## If you have already signed

All HK insurance policies carry a statutory 21-day free-look period from the date of policy delivery, during which the policy can be cancelled with a full refund of premiums paid. If a QDAP policy has been recently issued, cancellation is still possible within this window without financial penalty.

## The verdict

Skip QDAP for now. Route the HK$60K annual deduction into MPF TVC directed into a low-fee global index fund — specifically the DIS Core Accumulation Fund if your scheme offers it. Invest remaining savings in VWRA, with an optional small-cap value tilt via ZPRV and ZPRX. Keep 6–12 months of expenses in liquid savings entirely separate from the investment portfolio — this is the buffer that prevents forced selling during a market downturn or a career disruption.

Begin a gradual de-risking at age 45–48. Revisit QDAP seriously at 50–52.

The tax saving is worth having. The annuity is not the right vehicle for it — not yet.

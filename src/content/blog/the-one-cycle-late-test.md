I used to have five daily rituals. Morning brief, start-of-work priorities, midday reflection, end-of-work summary, bedtime close. Each one felt important when I designed it. Together they were homework.

The problem wasn't any single ritual — it was the frequency. I was checking things daily that only needed weekly attention. Reviewing investments in a morning brief. Pruning my task list every evening. Reflecting on career direction at bedtime. Each item passed the "is this important?" test but failed a different one: "what happens if I'm late?"

The heuristic that fixed it: **pick the longest cadence where being one full cycle late is still fine.**

If your portfolio sits unchecked for an extra week, does anything bad happen? No — make it monthly. If an email sits unread for an extra day? Maybe — keep it daily. If your career direction goes unexamined for an extra quarter? Probably fine — make it quarterly.

The one-cycle-late test biases toward less frequent. That's the point. A review you actually do every two weeks beats one you skip and feel guilty about every day. Frequency is a cost, not a virtue.

Two refinements that fell out of applying this:

**Trigger fidelity matters more than timing.** A routine attached to an existing behavior (the bus ride home, the first coffee) survives. One that needs a phone alarm survives less well. One that requires willpower ("I should remember to do this on Saturdays") dies. Match the importance of the review to the reliability of its trigger.

**Lead with forward, not backward.** Cal Newport's sharpest insight: a weekly review that starts with "what happened?" produces satisfaction. One that starts with "what are the three things that matter next week?" produces action. Reflection serves planning, not the other way around.

Five daily rituals became four cadences. The daily one runs on the bus home — inbox, messages, tomorrow prep. The weekly one runs Sunday afternoon — plan the week, quick backward glance. Monthly is maintenance. Quarterly is direction. Nothing at bedtime. Nothing before work. Nothing that requires willpower to start.

The system catches everything at the right frequency. I just live my life.
